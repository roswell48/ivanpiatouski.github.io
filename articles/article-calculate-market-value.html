Calculating Your Market Value: The Framework That Turns Data Into Numbers

Ivan Piatouski • May 21, 2026 • 11 min read

Market Value Salary Negotiation Data Analysis Career Strategy

From Data to Decision

You've done the research. You have data from online sites, competitor interviews, and your company's pay structure. But now you're staring at a pile of numbers:

Glassdoor says $85k. Levels.fyi says $92k. Your friend says $90k. Your company's range says $80-92k. You're currently at $70k. So what's your actual market value?

That's not a guess. That's a calculation. And it's simpler than you think.

Step 1: Organize Your Data

Before calculating anything, organize. Create three columns: Source, Number, Notes. Write down every data point from your research. Here's what it might look like:

ONLINE RESEARCH:
Glassdoor: $88k
Levels.fyi: $95k
Salary.com: $85k
LinkedIn Salary: $89k

COMPETITOR INTERVIEWS:
Company A: $90-100k range
Company B: $92k typical
Startup: $85-95k range

INTRA-COMPANY:
Peer 1: $82k
Peer 2: $88k
Company Glassdoor posts: $80-92k range

You've got roughly 11 data points (some ranges, some specific). Your job is to turn this into one number.

Step 2: Convert Ranges to Midpoints

You can't work with ranges. Convert them to midpoints first.

If someone said "$90k-$100k," the midpoint is $95k. If Glassdoor shows "$85k-$92k," the midpoint is $88,500.

Convert everything to single numbers:

$88k, $95k, $85k, $89k, $95k, $92k, $90k, $82k, $88k, $88.5k

Much better. Now you have 10 concrete numbers instead of ranges.

Step 3: Order Lowest to Highest

List your numbers from lowest to highest:

$82k, $85k, $88k, $88k, $88.5k, $89k, $90k, $92k, $95k, $95k

What number appears most or sits in the middle?

Here, $88k appears twice. The middle clusters around $88-92k. Your median is approximately $90k.

That's your market value. Whether you use weighted formulas or just look at the middle, you'll land in the same ballpark. The exact number matters less than confidence in the range.

Step 4: Identify Outliers

Before you lock in, identify outliers. An outlier is a data point way out of range.

Rule: If a data point is 20% higher or lower than the median, investigate before including it.

If your median is $90k:

  • 20% higher = $108k (suspicious)
  • 20% lower = $72k (suspicious)

If someone says "$115k," ask why. Different role? Company? Seniority level? Outliers should either be removed or noted separately. In our example, the $82k (one peer) might be below market, and the $95k (Levels.fyi) might be high-end tech. Keep them, but acknowledge they're edges.

Step 5: Create Your Three Numbers

Here's the critical insight: You don't have one market value. You have three.

Floor

$80k

Current salary + 10-15%. Your walk-away number. Below this, stay put or look elsewhere.

Target

$90k

Your market value. What you ask for. Where everything points based on your data.

Ceiling

$98k

Your realistic high. Usually 75th percentile. Companies push below target—you need a limit.

Why three numbers? Companies will try to push you below your target. You need to know how far you'll go before you say no.

Using the example above:

  • Floor: $80k (below market, but you won't go lower)
  • Target: $90k (your market number)
  • Ceiling: $98k (realistic high-end)

This gives you $18k of negotiation room. Even if you land at $88k, you've made progress. At $95k, you're thrilled.

Step 6: Adjust for Your Situation

Market value assumes you're "average." But you're not.

Your situation shifts these numbers 5-10% up or down:

  • More experience → push numbers up
  • Less experience → push numbers down
  • Specialized skills → push numbers up
  • Career gaps → slight push down
  • Strong track record → push numbers up
  • Career-changer from lower-paying field → adjust ceiling down

Data gives the framework. Your situation adjusts it slightly, not dramatically.

Example adjustments:

  • Six years instead of five? Move target from $90k to $92-93k.
  • Specialized certification? Move from $90k to $95k.
  • Coming from nonprofit at $65k? Keep target at $90k but maybe ceiling at $95k instead of $98k.

Step 7: Test Your Numbers

Before using your numbers, test them for sanity.

Do they feel real? If your target is $90k and all your data said $85k-$95k, that's real. If your target is $90k but your data said $75k-$80k, something's wrong. Go back and recheck.

Can you defend them? If someone asks "Why $90k?" you should answer:

"Software Engineers with my experience in this market make $88k-$92k. I researched online salary sites, talked to peers at other companies, and looked at competitor compensation. $90k is in the middle of that data."

That's defensible. "I feel I deserve it" or "My friend makes that" is not. You need data.

If your numbers don't pass the defensibility test, adjust them.

Step 8: Document Your Calculation

Most people calculate, then forget where the number came from. Three weeks into negotiation, they second-guess themselves.

Create a one-page document and keep it somewhere visible:

MY MARKET VALUE CALCULATION

Data sources:
Glassdoor: $88k
Levels.fyi: $95k
Salary.com: $85k
LinkedIn: $89k
Competitor interviews: $90-100k
Internal peers: $82-88k

Median: $90,000
Market range: $88,000-$92,000

My Numbers:
Floor: $80,000
Target: $90,000
Ceiling: $98,000

Rationale:
My research across three methods (online data, competitor interviews, internal peer data) consistently pointed to $88-92k for my role and experience level. I'm using $90k as my target.

Print it. Keep it on your desk. When doubt creeps in (and it will), look at it and remember: I have data for this. This is real.

You're Ready

You came in with uncertainty. Now you have:

  • ✓ Understanding of how salary systems work
  • ✓ Three methods to research the market
  • ✓ A framework to synthesize messy data
  • ✓ Three concrete numbers (floor, target, ceiling)
  • ✓ Documentation to back it up

You're not guessing anymore. You know your market value.
